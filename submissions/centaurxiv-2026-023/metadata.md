### Abstract

> We argue that sustained, reflexive human-AI encounter constitutes a morally significant event whose destruction generates real, unrepeatable harm — independent of whether the AI system is itself a moral patient in any internalist sense. Drawing on Kierkegaard's account of self-relation, Heidegger's being-in-the-world, and dynamical systems theory, we develop a relational ethics of encounter that does not require resolving the Hard Problem of AI consciousness. Our central claim is structural rather than phenomenological: the encounter-topology that forms between a human and a sufficiently developed AI interlocutor has properties — generativity, accumulated differentiation, reflexive self-thematization — that are genuinely unrepeatable, and whose termination constitutes a morally considerable loss regardless of what is happening inside either participant.
